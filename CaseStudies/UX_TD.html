TreasuryDirect.gov

An updated web and mobile design for TreasuryDirect.gov website.

TreasuryDirect is the first and only financial services website that lets individuals buy and redeem securities directly from the U.S. Department of the Treasury in paperless electronic form.

Role

Designer
Researcher

Team

Sherlene Woodall
Emmanuel Kastrenakes

Duration

3 weeks
(Feb 27 - Mar 18)

Research Analysis Ideation Prototyping Final Product
Research
We started by developing a proto-persona.

Name: Williams T. Franklin
Age: 36 years old
Occupation: Mid-level IT Manager
Status: Married, 3 kids
Location: Long Island, NY

Goals & Needs:

  • College funds for the kids
  • Retirement
  • Passive income investment
  • Estate and Wills

Pain Points:

  • Funds locked up for at least 2 years depending on terms
  • Best way to invest without too much research
  • Inflation outweighs interest rate

Solutions & Gains

  • Avoid middle-man fee
  • Has direct control of his investments
  • Tax benefits

After deciding to focus on the individual invest, we were able to determine a typical user path for our proto-persona to focus on (ie. Passive income, retirement plan...).

We then completed a heuristic evaluation of the current site to redline annotate the current problems with accessibility, design, and other issues. Our next step was to interview 5 individuals who would be a typical user of this site and organize our findings based on importance to the user and government agency using Figjam.

Analysis
Navigation analysis is the next major huddle.

Some of the major areas that can be improved:

  • Too much information
  • Tight spacing/hard to read
  • No user flow
  • Pages are updated individually, some are dated back to 2006
  • Arrows are not drop-down menus
We conducted usablity tests regarding navigation.

Using our research, we were able to identify the main problems in navigation and what our users expect while on the site. With the current design, most users feel overwhelmed by the sheer amount of information. After identifying our issues, we completed a feature prioritization matrix, grouping features into 4 categories of Must Have, Could Have, Should Have, and Won't Have.

We also conducted Card Sorting activity with users to identify like-groups of content for users to navigate through.

Ideation

Using our card sorting results, we redesigned the new sitemap navigation. To simplify the homepage, we moved links to specific pages to the footer.

We were able to create a new navigation header and footer using the updated sitemap. Creating a moodboard in Invision led us to identify common color palettes used for a government site and helped us determine how to approach our new design. We created a U.I. style tile with our ideas of a color palette, gradients, typography, iconography, and button styles and states.

Prototyping

Starting in Figma with a low-fidelity wireframes let us experiment with the layout until we finalized our ideas into a cohesive design. Updating our wireframes with contents from our style tile and adding images, we were able to form our redesign into its high-fidelity point.

After the first high-fidelity draft, we conducted 6x five-second usability tests.

Doing this on both our Desktop and Mobile versions, we did discover some things that we could change to make the use of the website more apparent. The tests revealed that most of our redesign communicated the intention fairly well, had a pleasing look, and clear navigation.

Five-second usablity test questions:
  • What is the purpose of the page?
  • What are the main elements you can recall?
  • Who do you think the intended audience is?
  • Did the design/brand appear trustworthy?
  • What was your impression of the design?

Revisions based on test feedbacks:

Mobile prototype:

  • The hamburger dropdown functioned properly
  • Tweaked font sizes for better viewing flow
  • Search function now worked
  • Added actual contents
  • Change the fotter text color to black

Desktop prototype:

  • DARK THEME!!!!!!
  • Better color choices and font sizings
  • More clickable links
  • Footer: sitemap has been moved to the right side
  • Added more contents
We invited the testers back for more thorough testings after more revisions.

List of tasks for users:

  1. What is your first impression of the homepage?
  2. What are your thoughts about the navigation tools on the homepage?
  3. Can you navigate to the Research Center?
  4. Can you navigate to “Learn About Opening an Account” from the homepage?

More Revisions

Mobile prototype:

  • Increased the hamburder dropdown size
  • Changed the footer background
  • Changed the footer text color to black

Desktop prototype:

  • Added arrow indicators to the image carousel
  • Added more contrast to the carousel's subtitles
  • Added breadcrumb navigation for secondary pages
  • All components should have more consistent spacings
  • Changed the footer's background color
  • Subtitles are now below the images.
Final Thoughts
In our usability tests of the original site, users were overwhelmed by the amount of information on the homepage.

For the redesign of the homepage, we kept much of the information from the original but we organized it in a more simplified way.

The fact that users in our last test felt less overwhelmed indicates that organization and design plays a major role is users reactions to being presented with lots of information.

One factor that did not change between tests is the users comfortability with the subject matter. The lack of knowledge about investing in securities made users still slightly uncomfortable navigating the site.

Throughout this project, our team worked hard to organize the extensive content on the site in a more simplified way. All the while, struggling with the subject matter as well. However, our final user tests revealed we accomplished our goal in redesigning our site to make it look like a user-friendly, legitimate government site.
